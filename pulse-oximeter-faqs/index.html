Pulse Oximeter questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Pulse oximeters measure your blood oxygen level (SpO₂) and heart rate by passing light through your fingertip. For healthy adults, normal readings range from 95% to 100%; persistent readings below 92% warrant medical attention. Accuracy can vary by brand and movement, so choose a well-reviewed model and follow proper placement techniques.

How does a pulse oximeter work?

A pulse oximeter clips onto your fingertip and uses two light beams, red and infrared, to detect how much oxygen is in your blood. Oxygenated blood absorbs more infrared light, while deoxygenated blood absorbs more red light. The device calculates the ratio and displays your SpO₂ percentage, along with your pulse rate. The technology is called photoplethysmography, and it’s essentially the same method used in hospital monitors. Consumer fingertip models are smaller and battery-powered, but they rely on the same optical sensors. Getting a good reading requires the finger to be at heart level, still, and free of thick nail polish or dirt.

What is a normal oxygen saturation reading?

For most healthy people without underlying lung or heart conditions, a normal SpO₂ reading sits between 95% and 100%. Readings of 94% are considered borderline and may prompt a call to your doctor, especially if you have symptoms like shortness of breath. Any reading consistently below 92% or below 90% can signal hypoxemia and requires urgent medical evaluation. It’s worth noting that age, altitude, and chronic conditions can shift your personal baseline. People with COPD sometimes live with readings as low as 88% to 92%, but that should be established with their healthcare provider. Trends over time matter more than a single number, a sudden drop from 96% to 91% is more concerning than a stable 93%.

How accurate are consumer pulse oximeters?

Consumer pulse oximeters are generally reliable for spot-checks of oxygen saturation, especially when compared to hospital-grade equipment. The FDA has cleared many over-the-counter models, but clearance isn’t the same as rigorous clinical validation. A well-reviewed brand often delivers readings within 2% of a lab reference, meaning if the device says 97%, your true oxygen level is likely between 95% and 99%. Accuracy decreases with poor perfusion (cold hands), movement, or very dark nail polish. Less expensive models may have slower response times or wider error margins. If you truly need precise readings for a medical condition, ask your doctor for a prescription-grade pulse oximeter or one validated by studies. For everyday awareness, a consumer model in the mid-range price point is sufficient.

What features should I look for in a pulse oximeter?

Start with a clear, bright display, OLED screens are easier to read in dim light than LCD. Some models automatically adjust the reading orientation no matter which way the device faces, which is handy for self-checking. Look for a device that includes a perfusion index (PI), which tells you if the signal strength is good for an accurate reading. Battery life matters: most run on two AAA batteries for a long stretch of continuous use. Many budget-friendly models offer the same core accuracy as premium ones, but you pay extra for faster measurement, better case, and additional features like plethysmograph waveform. Avoid devices that make unsubstantiated claims about detecting lung diseases, no oximeter alone can diagnose COVID-19 or pneumonia.

How do I get the most accurate reading?

Remove any nail polish, especially dark colors, and warm your hands if they are cold. Place the oximeter on your middle or index finger, at heart level, and keep your hand steady. Wait 10 to 20 seconds until the reading stabilizes, don’t trust the first number that appears. If you see a blinking heart symbol or a low perfusion index, reposition the clip or try a different finger. Movement, talking, or even shallow breathing can cause fluctuations. For a reliable spot-check, take the reading after resting for five minutes. One reading is rarely enough; take three successive readings and average them. If the numbers vary widely, check your technique or try a different device.

Frequently asked questions

What does SpO₂ stand for?

SpO₂ stands for peripheral capillary oxygen saturation. It measures the percentage of hemoglobin in your blood that is carrying oxygen.

Is a reading of 95% normal?

Yes, 95% is within the normal range of 95% to 100% for healthy individuals. However, if you have respiratory symptoms or a consistent drop from your usual baseline, check with your doctor.

Do nail polish or artificial nails affect readings?

Yes. Dark nail polish, gel polish, and thick artificial nails can interfere with the light absorption and cause inaccurate readings. Remove polish from the finger you use, or use a side-reading position if polish can’t be removed.

Can I use a pulse oximeter on a child?

Many fingertip models are designed for adult fingers and may not fit small children properly. Look for a pediatric pulse oximeter or a model with a child mode. Always follow the manufacturer’s age recommendations.

How often should I check my oxygen level?

If you are healthy and just monitoring out of curiosity, once or twice a day is fine. People with chronic conditions like COPD or heart failure may be advised by their doctor to check several times daily, especially during symptoms.

In shortA pulse oximeter is a handy tool for tracking your oxygen levels and pulse, but it is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Use it to spot trends and notify your doctor of any concerning changes. Choose a reputable brand, follow the instructions carefully, and you’ll have a useful indicator of your respiratory health.
