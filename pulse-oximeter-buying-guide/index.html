How to choose the right pulse oximeter

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A pulse oximeter clips onto your finger to quickly measure blood oxygen saturation (SpO₂) and heart rate. The best choice for home use is a clinically validated fingertip model from a reputable brand like Masimo, Nonin, or Contec, with a clear display and reliable accuracy. Avoid cheap, unregulated devices, and always look for FDA clearance or equivalent certification.

What is a pulse oximeter and how does it work?

A pulse oximeter is a small device that shines two wavelengths of light through your fingertip to measure how much oxygen your blood is carrying. It reports your oxygen saturation (SpO₂) as a percentage, alongside your pulse rate. The technology is called spectrophotometry, and it works best on well-perfused areas like fingers or earlobes. Most home-use models are battery-powered and clip onto the index or middle finger. They display real-time readings and often include a waveform (plethysmograph) that helps you confirm a good signal. While they are not diagnostic tools, they are excellent for spotting trends and catching potential drops in oxygen levels early.

Key features to look for in a pulse oximeter

Accuracy is everything. Look for devices that have been independently tested and cleared by regulators such as the FDA or CE. Brands like Masimo and Nonin set the gold standard, with clinical-grade accuracy that is trusted in hospitals. Consumer brands such as Contec and Wellue also offer reliable options at a lower price point. A clear, bright display is essential, especially if you will use the device in dim light or need to read it quickly. Many modern oximeters have an auto-rotate screen that flips based on orientation. Also consider whether you need an audible alarm for low oxygen or high heart rate, which is useful for overnight monitoring. Battery life varies widely; some run on standard AAA cells for hundreds of readings, while others have rechargeable lithium batteries that may last days between charges.

Fingertip vs. handheld vs. wearable: which type fits your needs?

Fingertip pulse oximeters are the most common for home and travel use. They are lightweight, easy to store, and usually cost between very affordable and a few hundred dollars. For occasional spot checks, like during altitude hiking, flying, or mild respiratory illness, a fingertip model is all you need. Handheld oximeters are bulkier and typically used in medical settings or for continuous monitoring at home. They include probes with a cable and often offer more detailed data logging. Wearable pulse oximeters, like the Masimo W1 or the Wellue O2Ring, track oxygen all night long and are popular for sleep apnea screening. These are more expensive but give you trend data over hours, which is far more useful than single readings.

Understanding accuracy claims and the FDA loophole

A major problem in the consumer market is that many cheap oximeters on Amazon make bold accuracy claims but have never been independently verified. In the United States, pulse oximeters are class II medical devices, and manufacturers can self-certify or rely on a special 510(k) clearance that does not require rigorous testing against a blood gas reference. This loophole means a oximeter can legally claim &#8220;FDA registered&#8221; even if it is off by 4% or more. To get the truth, look for specific clinical validation studies or clear statements like &#8220;cleared by the FDA for medical use.&#8221; Brands that openly share their accuracy data, such as Masimo, Nonin, and Contec with their CMS-50 series, are far more trustworthy. A difference of a few percentage points can matter immensely when deciding whether to seek medical care.

How to use a pulse oximeter correctly for accurate readings

For the most reliable result, rest your hand on your chest at heart level, remove any nail polish or artificial nails, and keep the oximeter steady on a clean, dry finger. Wait 10 to 20 seconds until the reading stabilizes, often indicated by a solid pleth waveform and a steady number. Avoid reading the value immediately after inserting your finger; motion or poor positioning can cause a false low reading. If you get a reading below 92% at rest and it does not improve after a few minutes of deep breathing, it is worth contacting a healthcare provider. Remember that a single low number is not an emergency by itself, but a consistent downward trend over hours is important. Clean the sensor between uses with a soft cloth and alcohol to avoid skin oil buildup that can interfere with the light.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a pulse oximeter to check for COVID-19 or pneumonia?

Yes, pulse oximeters are widely used to monitor oxygen levels during respiratory illnesses. However, a normal reading does not rule out COVID-19 or pneumonia, and a low reading can be a sign of &#8220;silent hypoxia&#8221;. low oxygen without obvious shortness of breath. Always follow your doctor&#8217;s guidance, not just the device.

What is a normal oxygen level on a pulse oximeter?

A normal SpO₂ reading for a healthy person at sea level is between 95% and 100%. Readings between 92% and 94% may be acceptable for some individuals with chronic lung conditions, but you should consult your physician for personalized targets. Below 92% is considered low and warrants medical attention.

Do I need a prescription to buy a pulse oximeter?

No, over-the-counter pulse oximeters are widely available without a prescription. However, prescription-grade models (like those from Masimo and Nonin) often require a doctor&#8217;s order and are more expensive. For home monitoring, a reputable consumer model is sufficient for most people.

How accurate are cheap pulse oximeters sold online?

Many inexpensive oximeters from unknown brands have not been validated against clinical standards. Independent tests have found errors of 3% to 5%, especially at lower oxygen saturations. For reliable accuracy, choose a device with clear FDA clearance and published accuracy data, even if it costs a bit more.

Can I use a pulse oximeter on a child or infant?

Yes, but you need a device specifically designed for smaller fingers or a pediatric probe. Most adult fingertip oximeters do not fit children under about 40 pounds and can give inaccurate readings. Look for models that include a pediatric clip or a wrap-style sensor for infants.

What does the perfusion index (PI) mean on my oximeter?

Perfusion index (PI) is a measure of blood flow at the measurement site. A higher PI (above 1%) indicates strong pulse strength and generally better reliability of the SpO₂ reading. If the PI is very low, the oximeter may struggle to get an accurate oxygen saturation because the signal is weak.

In shortA pulse oximeter is a simple tool that can give you peace of mind or an early warning sign, but only if you choose one you can trust. Focus on validated accuracy, a clear display, and a design that matches how you plan to use it. Whether you need occasional spot checks or overnight trend tracking, spending a little more on a reputable brand will pay off in reliable readings when you need them most.
